Abstract

As the Arctic transitions toward a seasonally ice-free state, capturing the complex morphology of submerged sea ice is crucial for advancing models of ocean-ice-atmosphere heat exchange and monitoring under-ice habitats.

Currently, sub-surface ice mapping is predominantly achieved through acoustic sensors such as multibeam sonar. While sonar provides robust large-scale geometry, it lacks the spectral and high-resolution textural information required to record fine morphological features — a critical limitation in under-ice environments, where sparse visual features and degraded sensing conditions challenge the capture of detailed structural and optical surface properties.

We present a benchmark for vision-based under-ice 3D reconstruction using real-world data from the MOSAiC expedition and synthetic data generated in an Unreal Engine-based underwater robotics simulator. The benchmark evaluates Structure-From-Motion (SfM) and monocular depth estimation, with quantitative comparison enabled through cross-modal registration to upward-looking multibeam sonar.

1. A benchmark for evaluating vision-based 3D reconstruction methods against spatially aligned under-ice multibeam sonar data.

2. A cross-modal alignment procedure that defines a shared sensing region for quantitative comparison between optical and acoustic reconstructions.

3. A simulation pipeline in HoloOcean based on sonar-derived ice geometry for generating controlled multimodal under-ice datasets.

Methods

We evaluate two complementary paradigms for vision-based 3D reconstruction against multibeam sonar ground truth from the MOSAiC expedition, using a shared cross-modal alignment procedure across real and simulated environments.

🧊

Structure-from-Motion (SfM)

Agisoft Metashape pipeline with ROV navigation priors for camera alignment, dense point cloud generation, and ICP-based cross-modal registration to the sonar reference.

🤖

Monocular Depth — DA3

Depth Anything V3 estimates per-frame depth maps and camera parameters from single images, enabling 3D reconstruction without explicit multi-view geometry.

🔊

Acoustic Ground Truth

Imagenex DT101 multibeam sonar at 240 kHz — 480 beams, 120° swath. Provides a physically grounded geometric reference unaffected by lighting or surface texture.

⚓

Cross-Modal Alignment

Manual coarse alignment followed by Iterative Closest Point (ICP) refinement. Evaluation restricted to the jointly observed region to avoid penalizing uncovered areas.

HoloOcean Simulation Pipeline

The HoloOcean framework mitigates the inherent noise and gaps present within field-gathered MOSAiC data. Using Unreal Engine's hardware-efficient rendering, we deployed an eight-motor ROV platform in a controlled environment with customized camera and sonar configurations.

An ice mesh was imported from real sonar survey data, with custom materials approximating the optical conditions at the ice-water interface. Water column parameters were calibrated to simulate subsurface light scattering with dense volumetric fog and non-uniform light attenuation. The simulated ROV carries a synchronized RGB camera and multibeam sonar matched to the MOSAiC hardware geometry and sampling rate.

Dataset — MOSAiC Expedition

The primary dataset was collected during the Multidisciplinary Drifting Observatory for the Study of Arctic Climate (MOSAiC) expedition, providing both under-ice topography and image data acquired with a multibeam sonar system and a high-definition camera mounted on a remotely operated vehicle (ROV).

We prioritized survey PS122/3_39-20, conducted on May 5, 2020, which provided the most visually consistent and geometrically complete overlap between acoustic and optical sensor streams.

Acoustic System

The Imagenex DT101 multibeam sonar operates at 240 kHz, emitting 480 beams simultaneously with a 120° across-track swath and 3° along-track beam width. The ROV was typically operated at 20 m below the ice for standard surveys (10 m for high-resolution passes). Standard processing yielded ~0.5 m grid resolution; high-resolution products achieved ~0.1 m lateral resolution.

Optical Processing

Videos required preprocessing to remove navigation overlays and watermarks. Upward-looking ROV photos were selected as the primary reconstruction input. Image enhancement (sharpening and haze reduction) was applied as a domain-alignment step for DA3, which is primarily trained on terrestrial imagery.
